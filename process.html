What’s in a process?

Or, cognition as a craft. 

Successful product design is the practice of turning [both learned and assumed] user behavior, into an interactive experience, through reverse-engineering the cognitive cycles a person goes through when using a product. Here’s kind of how I do that.
Prelude: Twenty years is a long time. With over 20 years as a design professional under my belt, and a fairly broad scope of experiences and interests, my most used skill is by far the ability to adapt and to bring design thinking in to a never-ended variance of existing processes, team structures, leadership styles, and company cultures. As a result, my ‘process’ is a moving target and in a constant state of evolution. Some people don’t like to hear this, due to the obsession with the false idea that ‘process’ is a magic tool that makes everything work perfectly, which came mostly out of the [dying] agency model of marketing and selling some kind of cryptic, unfailing process to organizations that didn’t have internal design teams.  Luckily, we all know better now. So let’s take a look at roughly how I’ve seen good products get built over the years: Part 1, Discovery In a perfect world, at least one member from every single team in the orgnization should be here, in addition to the key stakeholders who should be present at every core discussion. If you’re the janitor and have an interest in what we’re building, I want you in this conversation.  This should be treated as a limitless discussion, about your wildest dreams. Regardless of the conversation being about the entire product if we’re at a very early stage, or if it’s just a new feature that we’re thinking about adding to an existing product that’s been around for years: I want to know how crazy you’re willing to get with the concept.   The takeaway for the designer and/or product team, should be a massive collection of ideas that they can then take back to their headphones, meditations in the mountains, a ballsy dose of mushrooms, whatever their process is, and unpack it. This is not a meeting about technical limitations or solutions. Just ideas and goals. Personally, I like to see these go all day. Sometimes two or three days.  Part 2, Research Everything post-discovery, should always include qualifying and quantifying the ideas that came out of the discovery phase. Continuous validation is key to successful product. Is anyone else doing xyz? Does x makes sense? What about y? Does z add value to x and y, or does that need a deeper discussion? Typically, this involves market research, competitor analysis, thinking about how it fits in to the broader product (if one exists), or just how it fits in to the landscape of products being used by the target audience. Being a technologist at heart, and with my background in engineering and development, I tend to take my research beyond the traditional scope of “design research,” and also begin thinking about the technology stack that we might need to use to get xyz executed. If this is an existing product with existing users, it would behoove us to talk to our power users, to get ahead of potential friction as well as get some initial user insight in to how this new feature might be used. If this is a brand new product, it’s usually not a terrible idea to start talking to potential users. This isn’t always reasonable to do.
